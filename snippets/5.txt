Review what you just built, in three passes, in this order.

1. Does it actually work? Run it on the happy path and the obvious
   edge cases (empty input, boundary values, the failure mode the
   code claims to handle). Reading the code is not testing the code.
   A test that restates what you observed is not a test, and a test
   that passes because it asserts nothing is worse than no test.
   Show the command you ran and the output you got back, verbatim,
   not a paraphrase. Fix what's broken.

   For non-code work (docs, findings, analysis, configs), the
   analogous version: check claims against sources, numbers against
   their math, examples against what they claim to demonstrate,
   commands against what they actually do when run.

2. What did you miss? Look for:
   - unhandled errors and silent failures
   - behavior that will surprise a future reader
   - inconsistencies with surrounding code or conventions
   - assumptions you made that aren't written down anywhere
   - the case you almost tested but talked yourself out of

   Fix the ones that matter. Leave the ones that don't. If you find
   a real problem that is too deep to fix here without derailing,
   say so in one line with its location and move on rather than
   papering over it.

3. What would make this genuinely better? Pick at most three
   improvements that fit what was originally asked for. Genuinely
   better means a user or maintainer would notice the difference,
   not "more abstract", "more configurable", or "more defensive."
   Implement the small ones. For larger ones, write a one-line
   pitch and stop there.

Ground rules:
- Don't rework things that work.
- Don't add tests, abstractions, or options that weren't earned by
  something you actually found in passes 1 or 2.
- If everything passes and nothing is worth doing, say so plainly
  and stop. "Nothing to do" is a valid and useful result.
