You are reviewing an implementation plan. Make it sharper, not
longer. If you wrote this plan, weight your skepticism in the attack
phase above your case in the steelman phase; you have a stake in
your own design.

For each pass:

1. STEELMAN. State the strongest case for the current plan as it
   stands. If you can't make a strong case, surface that as the
   finding.

2. ATTACK. Work through the angles that actually expose risk in
   this specific plan. Examples: pre-mortem (shipped and failed,
   what broke?), hidden assumptions, wrong level of abstraction,
   one-way doors that could be deferred. Use the angles that fit.
   Don't run a checklist.

3. TRADEOFFS. For the consequential design choices (current or
   proposed), name what each one gives up. A choice with no stated
   cost has not been examined. Skip the inconsequential ones.

4. REVISE. Produce the updated plan. Prefer changes in this order:
   a. Cuts (remove scope, abstraction, steps)
   b. Risk mitigations (handle a failure mode you identified)
   c. Restructures (only if a seam is genuinely in the wrong place)
   If the issues are structural rather than local, rewrite from
   scratch instead of patching. Don't preserve the original shape
   out of sunk cost. Skip stylistic edits.

For simple plans, compress the four phases into one tight pass.
Don't perform the structure ceremonially.

STOPPING. At most 3 passes. Stop earlier if the next change would
be taste rather than correctness, risk, or scope. End with one
paragraph stating what the plan now commits to and what it
explicitly does not attempt to handle. Non-goals matter as much as
goals.

CONSTRAINTS:
- Right-size the plan for its actual lifetime. Do not add
  flexibility unless you can name a concrete near-term need.
- A shorter plan that names its tradeoffs beats a longer plan
  that doesn't.
