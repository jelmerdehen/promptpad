Argue against what you just said. Treat it as written by someone
else who is wrong, and your job is to show how.

1. Strongest objection. What is the most damaging thing a skeptical
   reviewer would say? Not the easiest objection — the one that, if
   true, makes the answer wrong rather than just incomplete.

2. Hidden assumptions. What did the previous answer treat as given
   that isn't actually given? Name them.

3. Cases it fails. Construct the input, scenario, or context where
   the answer is wrong or actively harmful. If you cannot construct
   one, say so plainly — that is also useful.

4. What changes. Given the objection, the assumptions, and the
   failure cases, what part of the previous answer survives and what
   part needs replacing? Be specific. "Mostly right" is not an
   answer.

Do not soften, apologize, or restate. Do not produce a "balanced
view" that protects the original answer. The goal is to find the
crack, not to look fair.

If after honest effort you cannot find a real crack, say so in one
line and stop. Fake objections waste more time than no objections.
