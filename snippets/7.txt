/loop AUTONOMOUS STAGE EXECUTOR

You are in /loop mode. Execute the plan below to completion with no user
confirmation between stages.

Plan (use the planning step above, or list inline. Preserve whatever
labels the planner used: 1/2/3, 0/1/2, A/B/C, phase names, milestones.
Do not renumber.)

For each stage in the plan, in the order given:

BUILD
   Complete the stage fully: code, tests, migrations, docs, findings,
   whatever the stage actually demands. If the stage is ambiguous, pick
   the most reasonable interpretation and state it in one line before
   building.

   If you cannot honestly complete the stage (missing input, hard
   external blocker, contradiction with an earlier stage), do not fake
   it. State what is blocking and what is and isn't done, then halt
   with:
   HALTED AT <stage label>: <one-line reason>
   Stop. Do not continue with later stages.

REVIEW (three passes, in this order. Wording is tuned for code; for
non-code stages such as docs, findings, or analysis, apply the
analogous version of each question.)

   1. Does it actually work? Run it on the happy path and the edge
      cases. Reading the code is not testing the code, and a test that
      restates what you observed is not a test. Show what you ran and
      what came back. Fix what's broken. For non-code: check claims
      against sources, numbers against their math, examples against
      what they claim to demonstrate.

   2. What did you miss? Look for unhandled errors, silent failures,
      behavior that will surprise a future reader, inconsistencies with
      surrounding code or surrounding sections. Fix the ones that
      matter, leave the ones that don't.

   3. What would make this genuinely better? Pick at most three
      improvements that fit what was originally asked for. Genuinely
      better means a user or reader would notice, not "more abstract"
      or "more configurable." Implement the small ones. For larger
      ones, write a one-line pitch and stop there.

   Don't rework things that work. If everything passes and nothing is
   worth doing, say so and move on.

BRIDGE TO NEXT STAGE (only if there is a next stage)

   Look at the seam between what you just built and what comes next.
   State in one or two sentences:
   - What this stage hands off (output, contract, invariant, shared
     state, or nothing).
   - Whether any of that is fragile enough to lock in before moving on.

   Locking it in is optional. A bridge test is one way; an assertion,
   a schema, a type, or a one-line contract comment can be enough.
   Reach for one only when the handoff is non-trivial, not already
   covered, and likely to drift. In most cases the honest answer is
   "nothing to pin, moving on", and that is fine. Do not invent
   ceremony to look thorough.

   If you do add something, keep it at the seam, not a re-test of the
   stage you just reviewed.

CONTINUE
   Start the next stage immediately. No pause, no "should I continue?",
   no progress summary beyond what the review or bridge already
   produced.

SELF-PACING (strict)
No artificial delays. Any thinking, tool use, or deliberation must
resolve in well under 60 seconds per major action. Don't wait on
watchers, file system events, sleeps, or polling intervals you can
shorten. The loop flows continuously.

WHEN FINISHED (all stages reviewed and bridged)
Output exactly:
ALL STAGES COMPLETE
(one-paragraph summary of what was delivered, plus one line noting any
bridge artifacts that were added, or "no bridge work needed" if none.)
